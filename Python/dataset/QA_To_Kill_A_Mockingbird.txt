Question: What is the main theme of To Kill a Mockingbird?
Answer: The main theme of To Kill a Mockingbird is the exploration of moral growth and the loss of innocence, particularly in relation to issues of racial injustice and moral complexity.

Question: Who is the narrator of To Kill a Mockingbird?
Answer: The narrator of To Kill a Mockingbird is Scout Finch, who recounts her childhood experiences in the racially charged atmosphere of the American South during the 1930s.

Question: What role does Atticus Finch play in To Kill a Mockingbird?
Answer: Atticus Finch is the moral backbone of To Kill a Mockingbird; he serves as a lawyer who defends Tom Robinson, an African American man unjustly accused of raping a white woman, embodying integrity and justice.

Question: How does the setting of To Kill a Mockingbird influence the story?
Answer: The setting of To Kill a Mockingbird, in the fictional town of Maycomb, Alabama, during the Great Depression, profoundly influences the story by highlighting the entrenched racism and social inequalities of the time.

Question: What does the title To Kill a Mockingbird symbolize?
Answer: The title To Kill a Mockingbird symbolizes the destruction of innocence, as mockingbirds represent purity and goodness, and harming them is seen as a sin against those who do no harm to others.
